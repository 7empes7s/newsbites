OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google are sharing intelligence on attempts to copy frontier AI models through the Frontier Model Forum, the industry group they co-founded with Microsoft in 2023. Bloomberg says it is the first coordinated defense effort between the three labs.

## What triggered the response

Anthropic says DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax used about 24,000 fake accounts to generate more than 16 million Claude exchanges. The goal, according to Anthropic, was to extract Claude's capabilities and use the outputs as training data for their own models.

The scale varied significantly across the three firms. MiniMax alone accounted for roughly 13 million of the 16 million exchanges, about 81 percent of the total. Moonshot AI generated 3.4 million more. DeepSeek contributed a smaller volume but reportedly employed the most technically sophisticated methods, according to VentureBeat's reporting on Anthropic's data.

## What adversarial distillation actually is

Adversarial distillation means repeatedly querying a stronger AI model and using the answers to train a cheaper one. The result is a model that can reproduce much of the original capability without paying for the research, compute, and talent that created it.

The technique is not new, but the scale documented by Anthropic — tens of millions of exchanges routed through thousands of fake accounts — represents an industrial-grade operation rather than casual misuse.

## Why competitors are cooperating

The Frontier Model Forum was created to address shared AI safety risks. It is now also being used to share intelligence on model-copying attacks, which suggests the labs no longer think they can handle the problem alone.

Each company has its own terms of service prohibiting distillation, and each has its own detection systems. But because the same actors target multiple labs, sharing intelligence on tactics and account patterns makes every lab's defense stronger. A pattern detected by Anthropic can be flagged to OpenAI and Google before the same actors attempt the same technique on their models.

## What remains unclear

The public data so far comes primarily from Anthropic. OpenAI and Google have not disclosed specific numbers about distillation attempts against their own models. It is also unclear whether Microsoft, a Frontier Model Forum co-founder, is participating in this specific intelligence-sharing initiative.

The specific countermeasures the Forum will deploy beyond information sharing have not been publicly detailed. And the broader legal and diplomatic implications of naming specific Chinese companies remain open — the disclosure sits at the intersection of commercial IP protection, export controls, and US-China technology competition.

## Why it matters

This is the first time three direct competitors have publicly pooled intelligence to stop large-scale AI model copying. That alone shows how seriously the labs now view the threat.

The decision to cooperate suggests that adversarial distillation has moved from an annoyance to a structural threat to the business model of frontier AI development. If building a frontier model costs billions but copying its outputs costs a fraction of that, the incentive to invest in frontier research weakens. The labs appear to have concluded that defending against that dynamic individually is not enough.
