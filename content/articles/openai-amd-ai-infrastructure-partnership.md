OpenAI has committed to a 6-gigawatt AI computing deployment with AMD, according to AMD's investor-relations announcement and a same-day regulatory filing, both dated October 6, 2025. The agreement starts with an initial 1 gigawatt deployment in the second half of 2026 using AMD Instinct MI450 series GPUs, and AMD has disclosed a warrant structure that gives OpenAI a financial stake in the chipmaker tied to how quickly the deployment proceeds.

The announcement confirms, in unusually specific terms, that OpenAI is actively diversifying the hardware layer of its AI infrastructure — and that AMD has secured one of the most significant disclosed customer commitments in the company's recent history.

---

## The Deal: What AMD Has Disclosed

The core terms of the agreement are drawn directly from AMD's official press release and a Form 8-K filed with the SEC on October 6, 2025.

AMD said the total agreement covers 6 gigawatts of AMD GPU-based computing capacity. The first phase — 1 gigawatt — is scheduled for deployment in the second half of 2026 using AMD Instinct MI450 series GPUs. The remaining 5 gigawatts are outlined in the agreement but AMD has not publicly specified a timeline or confirmed which GPU generations would be used for those later phases.

AMD described the deal as "expected to generate tens of billions of dollars in revenue" over its lifetime. That framing is company guidance from AMD, not a contractually disclosed minimum or a guaranteed outcome. The full commercial terms — including pricing assumptions, deployment schedules for each gigawatt tranche beyond the first, and cost structures — have not been made public. Readers and investors should treat the revenue figure as an expression of AMD's internal expectations, not a committed figure.

The 8-K filing provides greater specificity on the financial mechanics of the partnership. Under the warrant agreement, AMD granted OpenAI rights to purchase up to 160 million AMD shares at an exercise price of $0.01 per share. The warrants are exercisable until October 5, 2030. Vesting is structured in six tranches, each corresponding to a 1-gigawatt deployment milestone — meaning OpenAI's ability to exercise warrants is directly linked to how much AMD hardware it actually puts into production. AMD also disclosed that vesting is conditioned on AMD share price targets that escalate up to $600 per share.

The warrant structure is notable for several reasons. It aligns OpenAI's financial incentives with AMD's business success, creates a multi-year accountability mechanism for deployment progress, and ties a meaningful potential equity stake to real infrastructure outcomes rather than simple financial commitment. CNBC reported that if fully vested and exercised, the warrant package would represent approximately 10 percent of AMD's outstanding shares at the time of the announcement.

## What 6 Gigawatts Means

Gigawatts, in the context of AI computing agreements, refer to the total power capacity of the data centers deploying the hardware — not energy generation. A single large AI training cluster might consume tens or hundreds of megawatts; 6 gigawatts represents a scale that would, if deployed across multiple facilities, constitute one of the largest AI infrastructure programs publicly disclosed.

For context, hyperscale data center campuses that support large-scale AI workloads typically operate in the range of several hundred megawatts to low single-digit gigawatts. A commitment to 6 gigawatts spread across a multiyear deployment implies a sustained build-out across multiple facilities, likely across multiple geographies, and a long-term infrastructure relationship rather than a one-time procurement.

AMD and OpenAI have not disclosed where the capacity will be physically located, how it maps to existing or planned OpenAI data center programs, or how it integrates with other compute sources OpenAI uses. Those details are not in the verified public record.

What is confirmed is the initial tranche: 1 gigawatt of AMD Instinct MI450-based capacity, deployed in the second half of 2026. Hardware analysis reported by HotHardware suggests the MI450 series is AMD's next-generation AI accelerator, expected to compete with Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform. The MI500 series, a further generation, is not expected until 2027 — which suggests that if later phases of the OpenAI agreement ramp into 2027 and beyond, there is a reasonable expectation that future GPU generations would be incorporated, though this has not been confirmed by AMD.

## The MI450 Platform

AMD's Instinct MI450 series is the hardware platform confirmed for the first phase of the deployment. The MI450 represents AMD's latest generation of AI accelerator, succeeding the MI300 and MI350 lines and designed for large-scale AI training and inference workloads.

AMD has not published a full MI450 specification sheet in the verified sources available for this article. Secondary hardware analysis indicates the MI450 is positioned to compete in the training-focused segment of the accelerator market, where Nvidia holds dominant share. AMD's Instinct roadmap, as described in product announcements and analyst coverage, shows a two-year cadence: the MI450 was expected in 2026, with the MI500 to follow in 2027.

The fact that the OpenAI deal specifically calls out MI450 for the initial 1-gigawatt phase ties the deployment schedule to a specific AMD product cycle. Whether subsequent phases are tied to specific hardware generations has not been disclosed.

## Financial Terms and the Warrant Mechanism

The AMD 8-K filing is the most detailed public document on the financial terms of the partnership, and it warrants close reading.

The warrant agreement, filed as an exhibit, specifies that AMD issued to OpenAI the right to purchase 160 million shares of AMD common stock at $0.01 per share — effectively a near-zero exercise price, meaning the economic value of the warrant is almost entirely determined by AMD's stock price. At AMD's October 2025 trading range, 160 million shares at market price represented a substantial implied value.

Vesting occurs in six tranches, each tied to a specific deployment milestone corresponding to each gigawatt of deployment: the first tranche vests when 1 gigawatt of AMD hardware is deployed; the second at 2 gigawatts; and so on through the full 6-gigawatt commitment. Each tranche is also subject to AMD share price conditions, with price targets escalating up to $600 per share for later tranches.

The $600 share price target for later tranches is a meaningful threshold. AMD would need to appreciate substantially from its October 2025 levels for all six tranches to vest on schedule. This structure gives AMD a degree of protection: if AMD's stock does not perform well, OpenAI's ability to convert later warrant tranches into shares is constrained — or delayed until both the deployment milestone and the price condition are met.

The warrants expire on October 5, 2030, giving OpenAI a five-year window to complete deployments and exercise its position.

From AMD's perspective, the warrant mechanism is a tool for demonstrating committed long-term demand. A customer that holds equity warrants tied to deployment milestones has a direct financial incentive to actually deploy. For AMD, this reduces the risk that a large announced deal produces little actual revenue — a dynamic that has historically afflicted large infrastructure announcements that moved slowly from press release to production.

## Strategic Context: Diversification, Not Replacement

Multiple secondary sources, including CNBC and TechCrunch, placed the AMD deal in the context of OpenAI's broader hardware strategy. Reporting from CNBC as recently as February 2026 indicated that OpenAI and Nvidia were discussing a separate infrastructure deal estimated at approximately $100 billion, though that reporting also noted the negotiations had encountered delays.

The AMD commitment should not be read as OpenAI abandoning Nvidia. The verified record does not support that framing. AMD's own press release described the agreement as a "strategic partnership" and emphasized the complementary nature of the compute capacity. AI infrastructure at scale routinely involves multiple hardware vendors, and OpenAI's stated strategy has emphasized hardware diversification as a risk management approach.

What the AMD deal does confirm is that OpenAI is willing to make long-term commitments to AMD at a scale that is commercially meaningful to AMD. A 6-gigawatt agreement, even spread across multiple years, gives AMD a credible reference customer in the AI training and inference market — a market where Nvidia's H100 and subsequent GPU generations have been the dominant choice for large foundation model developers.

OpenAI's rationale for the AMD commitment, based on secondary sources and the reproduced elements of OpenAI's statement in the AMD press release, appears to center on supply security and capability breadth. The AI compute market has experienced significant capacity constraints in recent years. A long-term committed relationship with a second major GPU supplier reduces OpenAI's dependence on any single vendor and potentially improves its negotiating position with all of them.

## What AMD Gains

For AMD, the OpenAI deal is strategically significant independent of the revenue it ultimately generates.

AMD's Instinct line has gained commercial traction, but it has done so largely in workloads where customers could not obtain sufficient Nvidia supply or where AMD's software ecosystem, including ROCm, met their requirements. The OpenAI deal is a different kind of signal: a hyperscale AI developer choosing AMD at large scale under conditions where Nvidia supply — while constrained — was not absent.

AMD Chief Executive Lisa Su has publicly described the deal as a milestone for AMD's position in AI infrastructure. AMD's press release said the arrangement is "expected to be highly accretive to AMD's non-GAAP earnings per share," signaling that the financial terms are favorable to AMD even after accounting for the warrant dilution.

The six-tranche vesting structure, tied to deployment milestones, also means AMD's revenue from the deal is backloaded in a way that mirrors actual infrastructure buildout cycles. AMD recognizes revenue when hardware ships; if deployments proceed on schedule, the company should see a sustained multi-year revenue contribution rather than a single large order.

AMD also gains a reference customer for its enterprise and cloud sales efforts. The ability to point to OpenAI as a confirmed large-scale Instinct customer strengthens AMD's position with other AI developers evaluating whether to include AMD hardware in their own infrastructure plans.

## What Remains Unknown

The dossier behind this article, and the underlying public record, support the core claims: 6 gigawatts, 1 gigawatt in H2 2026 on MI450, 160 million warrants at $0.01, six-tranche milestone vesting, $600 share-price target for the final tranches, October 2030 expiry, "tens of billions" in expected AMD revenue.

Several important questions are not yet answered in the public record.

The hardware platform for the remaining five gigawatts is unconfirmed. Given AMD's roadmap, later phases could involve the MI500 or subsequent generations, but AMD has not disclosed this.

The physical locations for the deployments are not public. Whether the hardware will be deployed in OpenAI's own data centers, in facilities operated by Microsoft Azure (OpenAI's primary cloud partner), or in a combination of environments has not been disclosed.

The pricing structure — what OpenAI will pay per unit of compute capacity, and how that pricing compares with AMD's other enterprise customers or with Nvidia's equivalent offerings — is not public.

The relationship between this AMD agreement and OpenAI's Nvidia conversations is not formally documented. CNBC's February 2026 reporting described the Nvidia deal as stalled but not abandoned. Whether the AMD commitment affects the size, structure, or urgency of any Nvidia agreement is not confirmed.

OpenAI's official statement on the partnership was not directly accessible for this article — the URL returned an HTTP 403 error. The substance of OpenAI's statement has been reproduced in AMD's press release and quoted consistently across secondary reporting, and the overall narrative is consistent. But any direct quotes from OpenAI in this article would need to be sourced through AMD's reproduction of that statement or through verified secondary coverage, and none are included here.

## A Documented Foothold

The clearest conclusion from the verified record is also the most durable: AMD has secured a documented, multi-gigawatt infrastructure commitment from one of the most prominent AI developers in the world, backed by an SEC filing and a warrant structure that ties OpenAI's financial upside directly to deployment execution.

The scale of the deal — 6 gigawatts, tens of billions in AMD's expected revenue — reflects the magnitude of AI infrastructure investment now underway across the sector. Whether those investments ultimately produce the returns their backers expect is a question the market will answer over the next several years. What the AMD-OpenAI agreement confirms is that the capital is being committed, the hardware is being ordered, and the relationships that will shape the next generation of AI infrastructure are being locked in now.

AMD gets a flagship customer and a deployment timeline. OpenAI gets a large guaranteed hardware supply and a financial stake in the vendor delivering it. The warrant structure gives both companies an incentive to make the deployments proceed on schedule.

The remaining 5 gigawatts will be the story to watch.
