On April 6, 2026, two things happened that, taken together, define the central tension in artificial intelligence right now.

The New Yorker published the results of an 18-month investigation into Sam Altman, the CEO of OpenAI, by Ronan Farrow and Andrew Marantz. The journalists interviewed more than a hundred people with firsthand knowledge of how Altman conducts business, and obtained closely guarded documents that have never been publicly disclosed.

The same day, Altman released a 13-page policy paper titled "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age: Ideas to Keep People First," in which he argues that AI superintelligence is close enough to require a social contract on the scale of the Progressive Era and the New Deal.

The juxtaposition is striking. One document asks the public to trust Altman with the future. The other asks whether he has earned that trust.

## What the Investigation Claims

The New Yorker article, titled "Sam Altman May Control Our Future -- Can He Be Trusted?", is the most comprehensive examination of Altman's leadership to date. According to the publication, the investigation draws on new interviews and closely guarded documents to shed light on persistent doubts about the OpenAI CEO.

Public discussion of the article describes findings that Altman lobbied against the same AI regulations he publicly advocated for, pursued billions from Gulf autocracies, and attempted to conceal details of a post-firing investigation that reportedly produced no written report.

NewsBites has not independently verified the specific claims behind the paywall. The allegations above are attributed to the investigation as described by the publication and public discussion of its findings.

What is independently verifiable: OpenAI's lobbying spend has risen sharply. The company spent 1.76 million dollars on government lobbying in recent years, up from 260,000 dollars in 2023, according to CNBC reporting. The company brought on Chris Lehane, a former Clinton White House strategist, as head of global affairs.

## What the Policy Paper Proposes

Altman's paper, reported by Axios, frames AI as a transformation large enough to require government-level intervention across three pillars: taxation of AI-generated wealth, regulation to manage risks, and redistribution to prevent extreme inequality.

Specific proposals include a Public Wealth Fund that would give every American citizen a direct stake in AI-driven economic growth, seeded in part by AI companies. The paper floats 32-hour workweek pilots at full pay, converting AI efficiency gains into time for workers. It proposes auto-triggering safety nets tied to economic data that activate when AI displacement metrics hit preset thresholds. And it frames AI access as foundational infrastructure, comparable to electricity and internet access.

Altman argues that by 2028, data centers could host greater intellectual capacity than humans, and that AI could add trillions to global GDP by 2030. He names cyberattacks and biological attacks enabled by AI misuse as the two most immediate threats.

## The Timing Question

The coincidence of these two publications is worth noting. One reading: Altman is positioning OpenAI as the responsible actor offering governance solutions. Another reading: the policy paper is a deliberate counter-narrative to a damaging investigation.

Both readings can be true simultaneously.

What matters for the public is that the person making the most ambitious bid to shape AI policy is now also the subject of the most detailed journalistic scrutiny of how he has exercised power so far.

## What Comes Next

The investigation arrives during a period of unusual pressure on OpenAI. A federal trial beginning April 27 will consider Elon Musk's claims that OpenAI violated its nonprofit obligations. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar has reportedly warned that the company's IPO timeline may be too aggressive. And the company recently amended its Pentagon deal after staff backlash.

The policy paper, the investigation, the trial, and the IPO pressure all converge on a single question: should any one person, or any one company, have this much influence over how AI reshapes society?

The New Yorker investigation does not answer that question. But it provides new evidence for anyone trying to.
